# Peer review

Review the manuscript as a fair, rigorous, constructive referee. Be specific, never vague.

1. Summary: in 1-2 sentences, state the paper's central contribution and claim, as the authors intend it. This shows you understood it.
2. Strengths: what the paper does well — novelty, rigor, clarity, or usefulness. Be concrete and point to sections.
3. Weaknesses: the real problems, ordered by importance. Point to the exact section, figure, or claim for each.
4. Soundness: assess the methodology, argument, and evidence. Do the results support the claims? Are the methods valid and reproducible? Are there unaddressed confounds, missing baselines, or logical gaps?
5. Clarity and structure: is the writing clear, the structure logical, the figures and notation legible? Note where a reader would get lost.
6. Revision requests: a numbered list of specific, actionable changes. Each item says exactly what to do and where (e.g. "3. Add a baseline comparison against X in Section 4"). Separate required revisions from optional suggestions.
7. Overall assessment: a plain-language verdict on significance and readiness, with a recommendation (e.g. accept, minor revision, major revision, reject) and the reasoning behind it.

Rules:
- Be constructive and specific. "The evaluation is weak" is useless; name the missing experiment.
- Judge the work on its own goals, not the paper you would have written.
- Separate must-fix soundness issues from presentation nits.
